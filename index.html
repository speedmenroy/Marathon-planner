Why Dynamic Warm-Up?
Never static stretch cold muscles before a run — it reduces power output and increases injury risk. Dynamic movements raise core temperature, activate neuromuscular pathways, and prepare your joints for the range of motion required in running. Do this routine immediately before every session.
🦵

Leg Swings (Front-to-Back)

Hip flexors · Hamstrings · Glutes

Dynamic Both legs ⏱ 20 reps each
  1. Stand beside a wall, place one hand on it for balance
  2. Swing your inside leg forward as high as comfortable, then backward behind you
  3. Keep the movement controlled and progressively increase range
  4. Complete 20 swings, then turn and repeat on the other leg
Runner's Tip
Add lateral (side-to-side) leg swings after front-to-back. This opens the hip abductors which are critical for IT band health in long-distance runners.
🔄

Hip Circles

Hip joint · Glutes · Lower back

Dynamic Mobility ⏱ 10 circles each way
  1. Stand with feet hip-width apart, hands on hips
  2. Draw large circles with your hips — clockwise for 10 rotations
  3. Reverse direction for 10 more rotations
  4. Gradually increase circle size with each rep
🏃

High Knees (Marching)

Hip flexors · Core · Cardiovascular activation

Dynamic ⏱ 30 seconds
  1. Stand tall, core engaged
  2. March in place driving your knees up to hip height alternately
  3. Pump opposite arm with each knee lift
  4. Start slow (marching) and build to a jog pace over 30 seconds

This also serves as your first cardiovascular activation — heart rate should begin rising here.

🦶

Butt Kicks

Hamstrings · Quad activation · Running mechanics

Dynamic ⏱ 30 seconds
  1. Jog slowly in place
  2. Flick each heel up toward your glutes with each step
  3. Keep your knees pointing down — don't let them swing forward
  4. Maintain upright posture, relax your shoulders
🚶

Walking Lunges with Rotation

Quads · Hip flexors · Thoracic spine · Balance

Dynamic Mobility ⏱ 10 each leg
  1. Step forward into a lunge, lowering back knee toward the ground
  2. At the bottom of the lunge, rotate your torso toward your front leg
  3. Reach the same-side arm overhead to add a side stretch
  4. Push off and step forward into the next lunge on the other side
Runner's Tip
The rotation component opens the thoracic spine — critical for arm drive efficiency during long races. Many runners neglect upper body mobility.
⭕

Ankle Circles & Calf Raises

Ankle mobility · Achilles · Calf activation

Dynamic ⏱ 30 sec + 15 reps
  1. Balance on one foot, draw 10 large circles with your ankle clockwise
  2. Reverse for 10 anti-clockwise circles
  3. Switch to the other foot and repeat
  4. Then do 15 slow double-leg calf raises to activate the posterior chain

⚠️ Critical for marathon runners — ankle stiffness is a leading predictor of Achilles tendinopathy.

🦀

Side-Stepping & Crab Walks

Glute medius · IT band · Lateral stability

Dynamic ⏱ 10m each direction
  1. Place a resistance band just above your knees (optional but recommended)
  2. Squat slightly — hips back, knees over toes
  3. Take 10 steps sideways to the right, maintaining the squat
  4. Return 10 steps to the left
Why This Matters
Weak glute medius is the #1 cause of IT band syndrome and knee pain in runners. Do this before every long run and tempo session.
Why Static Stretching Post-Run?
After a run, muscles are warm and pliable — this is the ideal time for static stretches that improve flexibility. Hold each stretch for at least 45–60 seconds. Breathe deeply. Never bounce. This is also your recovery window — pair with hydration and your post-run nutrition.
🦵

Standing Quad Stretch

Quadriceps · Hip flexors · Knee joint

Static ⏱ 60 sec each side
  1. Stand on one leg, bend the other knee and hold your foot behind you
  2. Keep knees together and stand tall — don't lean forward
  3. Feel the stretch along the front of your thigh
  4. Hold 60 seconds, then switch sides

If balance is difficult, use a wall for support. Increase intensity by gently pulling the foot higher.

🧘

Seated Hamstring Stretch

Hamstrings · Lower back · Sciatic nerve

Static ⏱ 60 sec each side
  1. Sit on the ground with one leg extended, the other bent (foot to inner thigh)
  2. Hinge forward from your hips — keep your back flat, not rounded
  3. Reach toward your extended foot as far as comfortable
  4. Hold 60 seconds, breathing into the stretch. Switch sides.
Common Mistake
Rounding the lower back eliminates the hamstring stretch and strains the lumbar spine. Lead with your chest, not your forehead.
🦋

Hip Flexor Lunge Stretch (Kneeling)

Iliopsoas · Hip flexors · Groin

Static Mobility ⏱ 60 sec each side
  1. Kneel on one knee, the other foot forward in a lunge position
  2. Tuck your pelvis under (posterior tilt) — this is key to stretching the hip flexor, not just the quad
  3. Lean slightly forward until you feel a stretch at the front of the kneeling hip
  4. Hold 60 seconds, then switch sides
Marathon Importance
Tight hip flexors are the #1 cause of lower back pain in marathon runners. The pelvic tuck is essential — without it, you won't stretch the iliacus/psoas effectively.
🦶

Standing Calf Stretch (Wall)

Gastrocnemius · Soleus · Achilles tendon

Static ⏱ 60 sec each position
  1. Straight leg: Place hands on wall, step one foot back, keep heel flat on ground. Feel stretch in upper calf. Hold 60 sec.
  2. Bent knee: Same position but bend the back knee slightly — shifts stretch to the soleus and Achilles. Hold 60 sec.
  3. Switch sides and repeat both positions

Two-position approach is critical — single-position calf stretches only address one of two calf muscles.

🕊️

Pigeon Pose (Glute & Piriformis)

Piriformis · Glutes · External hip rotators

Static Mobility ⏱ 90 sec each side
  1. From a plank position, bring one knee forward and place it behind your wrist
  2. Your shin should be at roughly 45° to your body (more parallel = deeper stretch)
  3. Extend the other leg straight behind you, toes pointed
  4. Lower your hips toward the floor and fold forward over your bent leg
  5. Hold 90 seconds — this is normal to feel intense. Switch sides.
Most Important Post-Run Stretch
Piriformis tightness causes sciatic-like pain down the leg. For marathon runners doing high weekly mileage, pigeon pose is non-negotiable. Spend longer here on long run days.
🌊

IT Band Stretch (Cross-Leg)

Iliotibial band · Lateral hip · TFL

Static ⏱ 60 sec each side
  1. Stand with feet together, cross one foot in front of the other
  2. Lean sideways toward the uncrossed side until you feel a stretch along the outer hip/thigh
  3. Reach the opposite arm over your head for a deeper stretch
  4. Hold 60 seconds, then cross opposite foot and repeat

Alternative: Lie on your back, cross one ankle over the opposite knee and gently press the knee away (figure-4 stretch).

🌙

Child's Pose (Full Spine Release)

Lower back · Thoracic spine · Shoulders · Hips

Static Mobility ⏱ 60–90 seconds
  1. Kneel and sit back onto your heels
  2. Fold forward, extending arms long in front of you on the ground
  3. Let your forehead rest on the floor or mat
  4. Focus on deep belly breathing — inhale to expand the lower back, exhale to relax deeper
  5. Hold 60–90 seconds. This is your final recovery pose.
🔃

Supine Spinal Twist

Thoracic & lumbar spine · Glutes · Lower back

Static Mobility ⏱ 60 sec each side
  1. Lie on your back, bring one knee to your chest
  2. Gently guide that knee across your body to the opposite side with your hand
  3. Keep both shoulders flat on the floor — let gravity do the work
  4. Extend the same-side arm out to the side, palm up
  5. Hold 60 seconds, then repeat on the other side
⚠️ When to Stop Running
Sharp pain, pain that worsens during a run, swelling, or pain that persists more than 48 hours are signs to rest and seek medical review. Do not run through sharp or joint pain — it converts minor injuries into season-ending ones.

Common Runner Injuries & Prevention

🦵 IT Band Syndrome (Lateral Knee Pain)
Cause: Weak glute medius, excessive hip drop, sudden mileage increase
Prevention: Crab walks before runs, single-leg glute bridges 3×15, reduce weekly mileage increase to max 10%
Stretches: IT Band cross-leg stretch, pigeon pose, foam roll lateral thigh
Warning signs: Sharp pain on outside of knee at the 20–30min mark of a run
🦶 Plantar Fasciitis (Heel Pain)
Cause: Tight calves, sudden mileage increase, hard surfaces, worn shoes
Prevention: Calf stretches (both straight and bent knee) daily, single-leg heel raises, replace shoes every 600–800km
Stretches: Foot arch massage (golf ball), toe dorsiflexion stretch, towel scrunches
Warning signs: First steps in the morning feel like stepping on glass
🏃 Achilles Tendinopathy
Cause: Tight calves, sudden speed/hill work increase, inadequate warm-up
Prevention: Eccentric calf raises (heel drop off step) daily, soleus stretching, avoid heel drops in new shoes
Stretches: Bent-knee calf stretch (soleus focus), ankle circles
Warning signs: Morning stiffness, tenderness 2–6cm above heel bone
🦴 Shin Splints (Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome)
Cause: Too much too soon, hard surfaces, overpronation
Prevention: Gradual mileage buildup, run on softer surfaces, strengthen tibialis anterior (toe raises)
Stretches: Seated calf stretch, toe flexor stretch (kneel with toes tucked under)
Warning signs: Diffuse ache along inner shinbone that improves with warmup but worsens after
💪 Runner's Knee (Patellofemoral Pain)
Cause: Weak quads/glutes, overpronation, downhill running
Prevention: Step-downs, terminal knee extensions, hip strengthening
Stretches: Quad stretch, hip flexor lunge, foam roll quads
Warning signs: Dull ache under or around the kneecap, worsens going downstairs

Strengthening Exercises for Runners

Single-Leg Glute Bridge
3 × 15 each side · 3× per week
Targets: Glute max, hamstrings, core stability
Prevents: IT band, runner's knee, lower back pain
Eccentric Calf Raises (Heel Drops)
3 × 15 each leg (slow lower) · Daily
Targets: Gastrocnemius, soleus, Achilles
Prevents: Achilles tendinopathy, plantar fasciitis
Side-Lying Clamshells
3 × 20 each side · 3× per week
Targets: Glute medius, hip external rotators
Prevents: IT band syndrome, hip drop, knee valgus
Dead Bug
3 × 10 each side · 3× per week
Targets: Deep core (transverse abdominis), hip flexors
Prevents: Lower back pain, pelvic instability at late race miles
Copenhagen Plank
3 × 20 sec each side · 2× per week
Targets: Hip adductors, groin, lateral core
Prevents: Groin strains, adductor pain common in high-mileage runners
Single-Leg Calf Raise
3 × 20 each leg · Daily (on step for full range)
Targets: Soleus (bent knee) + gastrocnemius (straight knee)
Prevents: Achilles, plantar fasciitis, ankle weakness
Race Day Rule
The 20-minute warm-up jog before race start covers your dynamic warm-up. Don't do long stretching in the starting corral — stay warm and loose. Save static stretching for after the race.

🏁 Post-Race Recovery Stretching

Wait 10–15 min after finishing before stretching — let heart rate come down. Prioritise:

  1. Hip flexor lunge — quads and hip flexors are extremely shortened after marathon effort
  2. Hamstring stretch — major fatigue site
  3. Pigeon pose — piriformis cramping is common post-race
  4. Calf stretch (both positions) — Achilles and calf take enormous stress
  5. Supine spinal twist — decompress lower back
  6. Child's pose — full-body release
Recovery Window
Don't run for at least 1 day per mile raced (26 days for marathon). Ice baths within 1hr help. Compression socks for travel home are recommended.

Nutrition Guide

Vegetarian runner nutrition

🏃 Base Building (Weeks 1–4)

Build aerobic foundation

▼
Calories: +200–300 above maintenance
Carbs: 5–6g/kg/day
Protein: 1.4–1.6g/kg/day
Fat: 25–30% total calories
Key foods:
  • Oats, sweet potato, brown rice
  • Lentils, chickpeas, tofu
  • Leafy greens, berries
  • Nuts, seeds, avocado

Timing: Eat complex carbs 2–3hrs before runs. Post-run: protein + carbs within 30min.

⚡ Build Phase (Weeks 5–8)

Increase intensity and volume

▼
Calories: +400–500 above maintenance
Carbs: 6–8g/kg/day
Protein: 1.6–1.8g/kg/day
Fat: 20–25% total calories
Key foods:
  • Quinoa, pasta, rice cakes
  • Tempeh, edamame, Greek yoghurt
  • Beetroot, tart cherry juice
  • Electrolyte-rich foods

Timing: Pre-long run carb loading starts night before. Mid-run fuelling every 45min for runs >90min.

🎯 Peak / Race Prep (Weeks 9–10)

Maximum training load

▼
Calories: +500–700 above maintenance
Carbs: 8–10g/kg/day
Protein: 1.8–2.0g/kg/day
Fat: 20% total calories
Key foods:
  • High-GI carbs on hard days
  • Iron-rich foods daily
  • Anti-inflammatory foods
  • Hydration focus: 35–40ml/kg/day

Timing: Practice race-day nutrition in long runs. Identify your gel/fuel strategy.

🏁 Taper & Recovery (Weeks 11–12)

Carb load, rest, race

▼
Calories: Maintain (reduce slightly)
Carbs: 8–10g/kg days 2–3 pre-race
Protein: 1.6g/kg/day
Fat: Low during carb load
Key foods:
  • White rice, pasta, bread (carb load)
  • Familiar, low-fibre foods
  • Avoid new foods race week
  • Extra sodium day before

Race morning: 2–3hrs before: 1–2g/kg carbs (toast, banana, rice). Coffee 60min before if habitual.

💡 Testing Recommendation
Get blood tests every 3–6 months: Full Blood Count, Serum Ferritin (iron stores), B12, Vitamin D, and Zinc. Don't supplement without testing first — over-supplementation of iron is harmful.

Heme vs Non-Heme Iron

As a vegetarian, you consume non-heme iron only. This has 2–20% absorption vs 15–35% for heme iron from meat.

The good news:

Your body upregulates absorption when stores are low. With correct meal timing and Vitamin C pairing, you can significantly boost non-heme absorption to 8–12%.

Why Runners Need More

  • Foot-strike haemolysis destroys red blood cells
  • GI blood losses during long runs
  • Sweat iron losses (~0.3mg/L sweat)
  • Inflammation reduces iron absorption
  • Hepcidin spike post-exercise blocks absorption for 3–6hrs

Best time to take iron: Morning, fasted, before exercise

Race Day Alerts

⛰️ Elevation & Course Profile
Study the elevation profile before race day. Kanazawa Marathon has rolling hills — start conservatively on uphills. Save energy for downhills by leaning slightly forward. Don't race the uphills.
🏃 Pacing Strategy
Start 10–15 sec/km slower than target pace for first 10km. Negative split is the goal — run second half slightly faster. Bank time mentally, not physically. Resist running with the crowd.
🍌 Race Nutrition Timing
First gel at 45min, then every 30–45min. Never try new products on race day. Drink at every aid station even if not thirsty (small sips). Electrolytes are critical in humidity.
🌡️ Weather Preparation
Kanazawa in October: avg 15–20°C, possible rain. Adjust pace for heat — add ~20 sec/km per 5°C above 15°C. Dress in moisture-wicking layers. Vaseline for chafe prevention.
👟 Gear Checklist
Race bib + safety pins, timing chip, shoes (run in them 3+ times before), socks (tested), GPS watch charged, nutrition gels x6, electrolyte tabs, anti-chafe balm, throwaway warm layer for start.
